
# Week 5
Topic 1
qDebate Motion
§"Factories should reduce variability rather than add inventory to improve output."
qKey Concepts
§Variability, Inventory (WIP Buffer), VUT Equation: Cycle Time = Variability x Utilization x Process Time.
qPros (Affirmative)
§Core thesis: "Inventory is a tax on incompetence."
§Inventory masks root causes and removes urgency to fix unreliable processes. It carries hidden costs: carrying costs, longer lead times, delayed defect detection. Toyota proved that cutting inventory and exposing problems leads to superior performance.
qCons (Negative)
§Core thesis: "Variability is like gravity. You cannot eliminate it, you must engineer for it."
§Irreducible variability always exists in equipment, materials, and demand. Strategic buffers are engineering tools, not waste. Variability reduction has diminishing returns, and at some point inventory is the cheaper option.

Topic 2 - 
qDebate Motion
§"Zero Inventory should be treated as an aspirational target, not abandoned in favor of calculated buffer levels."
qKey Concepts
§Zero Inventory, Safety Stock / Buffer, Risk Exposure
qPros (Affirmative)
§Core thesis: "Comfort is the enemy of improvement."
§Zero Inventory as a direction forces teams to continuously expose and solve problems. Abandoning this target in favor of calculated buffers creates complacency and justifies overstock. Toyota's success came from relentlessly pursuing zero, not from accepting a "safe" inventory level.
qCons (Negative)
§Core thesis: "Math beats mottos."
§An aspirational target without quantitative grounding leads to reckless buffer cuts and real operational failures. Inventory decisions should be sized by data (demand variability, lead time, service level), not by ideology.

# Week 6
Topic 1- 
qDebate Statement
§"Should service companies treat high labor costs as a necessary investment for superior performance, or as an expense to minimize?"
qKey Concepts
§Service-Profit Chain, Labor as Investment vs. Cost, Cost Leadership
qPros (Affirmative)
§Core thesis: "Labor spending in service companies is an investment with measurable returns: lower turnover, better service quality, and stronger customer loyalty."
§Service delivery depends entirely on people. Underpaying leads to high turnover, inconsistent quality, and ultimately higher total costs. Costco and Trader Joe’s case.
qCons (Negative)
§Core thesis: "Labor is the largest controllable cost in service operations. Disciplined cost management, not higher spending, drives sustainable profitability."
§Process standardization and automation can deliver consistent service without premium wages. High labor costs compress margins and limit pricing flexibility. Walmart and Ryan-air case.

Topic 2- 
qDebate Statement
§"When demand exceeds capacity, how should a company respond: raise prices or expand capacity?"
qKey Concepts
§Demand-Capacity Gap, Pricing Power, ROIC, Market Share vs. Margin Trade-off
qPros (Raise Prices)
§Core thesis: "Capacity expansion increases fixed costs and long-term risk. Excess demand signals pricing power, and margin improvement contributes to ROIC more immediately and safely."
§Building new capacity requires heavy capital investment that cannot be reversed if demand shifts. Price increases capture value from existing demand without adding structural cost.
qCons (Expand Capacity)
§Core thesis: "Price increases risk customer defection and brand damage. In quality-driven markets, delivery speed and accessibility determine loyalty, and capacity expansion is the foundation for long-term market share."
§Customers facing higher prices and longer lead times will seek alternatives. Competitors who invest in capacity during shortage periods capture displaced demand permanently.

# Week 7
This week's discussion centers on a strategic decision-making exercise: whether to adopt TPS (Toyota Production System), and if so, how to implement it effectively. The discussion will examine this question through one of below  two distinct business lenses 
- High-mix, high-volume electronics manufacturer
- Service-oriented companies such as software development firms, retailers.

Unlike a prior pro/con debate, this session follows a format in which three participants actively develop and exchange their perspectives, while a dedicated Red Team member continuously challenges the arguments being made. The discussion will be conducted in a Fishbowl style, meaning participants may rotate in and out of the inner circle at designated points throughout the session.

Come prepared to articulate your stance on TPS adoption for each business context, think through viable implementation approaches, and engage with pointed counterarguments from the Red Team.
